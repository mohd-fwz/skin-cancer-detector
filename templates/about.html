Skin Cancer Types

Understanding the 7 types of skin lesions detected by our AI model

What is Skin Cancer?

Skin cancer is the abnormal growth of skin cells, most often developing on skin exposed to the sun. However, it can also occur on areas not ordinarily exposed to sunlight. Early detection is crucial for successful treatment.

Our AI model is trained to identify 7 different types of skin lesions using dermoscopic images from the HAM10000 dataset, which contains over 10,000 dermatoscopic images.

Early Detection Saves Lives
  • Skin cancer is the most common cancer worldwide
  • Early detection increases survival rate to 99%
  • Regular self-examination is recommended
  • Annual dermatologist check-ups are important

A simple guide to identify suspicious moles

A
Asymmetry

One half doesn't match the other half

B
Border

Edges are irregular, ragged, or blurred

C

Color is not uniform throughout

D
Diameter

E
Evolving

Changing in size, shape, or color

Melanoma (mel)

High Risk
What is Melanoma?

Melanoma is the most serious type of skin cancer. It develops in melanocytes (pigment-producing cells) and can spread to other parts of the body if not caught early.

Warning Signs:
  • Mole that changes in size, shape, or color
  • Asymmetrical mole with irregular borders
  • Multiple colors within the same lesion
  • Diameter larger than 6mm
  • Bleeding, itching, or crusting
Risk Factors:
  • Excessive UV exposure and sunburns
  • Fair skin, light hair, and eyes
  • Family history of melanoma
  • Many moles or atypical moles
  • Weakened immune system
Statistics

Survival Rate (Early): 99%

Survival Rate (Late): 27%

Annual Cases: 100,000+ (US)

Basal Cell Carcinoma (bcc)

Moderate Risk
What is Basal Cell Carcinoma?

The most common form of skin cancer. It begins in the basal cells and rarely spreads to other parts of the body, but can be locally destructive if not treated.

Warning Signs:
  • Pearly or waxy bump on face, ears, or neck
  • Flat, flesh-colored or brown scar-like lesion
  • Bleeding or scabbing sore that heals and returns
  • Pink growth with raised border and central indentation
Risk Factors:
  • Chronic sun exposure
  • Fair skin and light eyes
  • Age over 50
  • Radiation therapy history
  • Arsenic exposure
Statistics

Curability: Nearly 100%

Metastasis Risk: Very Low

Annual Cases: 3.6 million (US)

Actinic Keratoses (akiec)

Precancerous
What is Actinic Keratoses?

A precancerous skin growth caused by sun damage. While not cancer itself, it can develop into squamous cell carcinoma if left untreated. Also includes Bowen's disease (intraepithelial carcinoma).

Warning Signs:
  • Rough, dry, or scaly patch on sun-exposed skin
  • Flat to slightly raised patch or bump
  • Color variations: pink, red, or brown
  • Itching or burning sensation
  • Crusty or bleeding lesions
Risk Factors:
  • Frequent or intense sun exposure
  • Fair skin, blonde or red hair
  • Age over 40
  • History of sunburns
  • Weakened immune system
Statistics

Cancer Risk: 5-10%

Preventable: Yes

Treatment: Very effective

Benign Keratosis (bkl)

Benign
What is Benign Keratosis?

A non-cancerous skin growth that includes seborrheic keratoses, solar lentigines (age spots), and lichen planus-like keratoses. These are harmless but can sometimes be confused with skin cancer.

Characteristics:
  • Waxy, scaly, slightly raised growths
  • Color: tan, brown, or black
  • Appear "stuck on" to the skin
  • Common on face, chest, shoulders, or back
  • Usually painless
Key Points:
  • Not caused by sun exposure
  • Common in people over 50
  • Often hereditary
  • No treatment required unless cosmetic concern
  • Does not turn into cancer
Good News

Cancer Risk: None

Harmless: Yes

Common: Very

Dermatofibroma (df)

Benign
What is Dermatofibroma?

A common benign skin growth made up of fibrous tissue. It's harmless and often appears after a minor injury like an insect bite or splinter.

Characteristics:
  • Small, firm bump under the skin
  • Color: pink, red, gray, or brown
  • Usually less than 1cm in diameter
  • Dimples inward when pinched
  • Common on legs and arms
Key Points:
  • More common in women
  • May be itchy or tender
  • Remains stable over time
  • No treatment necessary
  • Completely harmless
Assessment

Cancer Risk: None

Removal: Optional

Recurrence: Rare

Melanocytic Nevi (nv)

Benign
What are Melanocytic Nevi?

Commonly known as moles, these are benign skin growths made up of melanocytes (pigment cells). Most people have 10-40 moles, which are usually harmless.

Characteristics:
  • Round or oval shape
  • Color: pink, tan, brown, or black
  • Flat or raised
  • Symmetrical with smooth borders
  • Uniform color throughout
When to Monitor:
  • If the mole changes in size, shape, or color
  • New moles appearing after age 30
  • More than 50 moles total
  • Moles larger than 6mm
  • Family history of melanoma
Information

Cancer Risk: Very Low

Normal: 10-40 moles

Monitoring: Recommended

üí° Tip: Self-examine moles monthly using ABCDE rule.

Vascular Lesions (vasc)

Benign
What are Vascular Lesions?

Benign skin growths involving blood vessels. This category includes angiomas (cherry angiomas), angiokeratomas, pyogenic granulomas, and hemorrhage (bleeding into the skin).

Types & Characteristics:
  • Cherry Angiomas: Bright red, dome-shaped bumps
  • Angiokeratomas: Dark red to black papules
  • Pyogenic Granulomas: Rapid-growing red bumps that bleed easily
  • Hemorrhage: Purple or red spots from broken blood vessels
Key Points:
  • More common with age
  • Usually harmless and painless
  • Can bleed if injured
  • Treatment for cosmetic reasons only
  • No cancer risk
